This quantum coin flip simulates a quantum superposition, where the coin can be in both heads and tails states simultaneously until measured.

The measurement collapses the superposition into one of the two states, demonstrating the principles of quantum mechanics.

In quantum computing, we use quantum bits (qubits) instead of classical bits. While a classical bit can only be 0 or 1, a qubit can exist in a superposition of both states at once.

The quantum circuit shown above uses a Hadamard gate (H) to create a superposition, putting our qubit in an equal probability of heads or tails. This is similar to how real quantum computers perform calculations.

When we measure the qubit, quantum decoherence occurs - the superposition collapses into a definite state according to quantum probability. This fundamental quantum behavior is what makes quantum computers potentially powerful for certain types of calculations.
